Candida glabrata - UTI PathoDB

Overview

Candida glabrata is a yeast species that is part of the normal human microbiota but can cause opportunistic infections, including urinary tract infections (UTIs). It is especially significant in elderly and immunocompromised individuals.

Characteristics

  • Unicellular yeast
  • Non-dimorphic (does not form true hyphae)
  • Part of normal flora of the gastrointestinal and genitourinary tracts
  • Inherently less susceptible to azole antifungals

Pathogenicity

Candida glabrata can cause UTIs, particularly in patients using urinary catheters, diabetic patients, and those receiving immunosuppressive therapy. Its ability to adhere to uroepithelial cells and form biofilms contributes to its pathogenicity.

Clinical Significance

C. glabrata is associated with funguria and invasive candidiasis. It demonstrates higher resistance to azole antifungals compared to other Candida species, necessitating careful antifungal selection based on susceptibility testing.

Treatment

Treatment includes the use of echinocandins or amphotericin B, especially in resistant cases. Fluconazole may be less effective due to intrinsic resistance. Removal of indwelling catheters is often necessary in UTI management.
