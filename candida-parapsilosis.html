Candida parapsilosis - UTI PathoDB

Overview

Candida parapsilosis is a yeast species commonly associated with human infections, particularly in hospitalized patients. It is an emerging pathogen in urinary tract infections (UTIs), especially in neonates and immunocompromised individuals.

Characteristics

  • Unicellular yeast
  • Forms pseudohyphae and rarely true hyphae
  • Part of normal human skin flora
  • Efficient biofilm producer, especially on prosthetic materials and catheters

Pathogenicity

Candida parapsilosis can adhere to indwelling devices such as catheters and form biofilms, leading to bloodstream and urinary infections. Its ability to thrive on parenteral nutrition solutions enhances its pathogenic potential in clinical settings.

Clinical Significance

C. parapsilosis is a notable cause of catheter-associated UTIs and bloodstream infections, particularly in neonates and patients in intensive care units. It exhibits reduced susceptibility to echinocandins compared to other Candida species.

Treatment

Treatment typically includes azoles like fluconazole or amphotericin B. Echinocandins may be less effective, so antifungal susceptibility testing is advised before initiating therapy. Removal of contaminated devices is also essential for successful management.
