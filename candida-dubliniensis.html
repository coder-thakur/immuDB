Candida dubliniensis - UTI PathoDB

Overview

Candida dubliniensis is a fungal species closely related to Candida albicans. It has been isolated from cases of oropharyngeal candidiasis, especially in HIV-infected individuals, and is also known to cause urinary tract infections (UTIs), particularly in immunocompromised patients.

Characteristics

  • Yeast-like fungus
  • Forms germ tubes and chlamydospores, similar to Candida albicans
  • Less virulent than Candida albicans
  • Commonly isolated in immunocompromised hosts

Pathogenicity

Candida dubliniensis primarily causes infections in individuals with weakened immune systems. Its role in UTIs is increasing, particularly in patients with catheters or those receiving broad-spectrum antibiotics. Biofilm formation contributes to its pathogenic potential.

Clinical Significance

C. dubliniensis is associated with fungemia, endocarditis, and UTIs. Infections are more frequent in patients with compromised immunity such as those undergoing chemotherapy, organ transplantation, or HIV/AIDS. It shows some resistance to azole antifungals, making susceptibility testing important.

Treatment

Treatment involves antifungal agents such as fluconazole, amphotericin B, or echinocandins. However, resistance patterns may vary, and antifungal susceptibility testing is recommended for effective therapy.
