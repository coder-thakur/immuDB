Human Papillomavirus (HPV) - UTI PathoDB

Overview

Human Papillomavirus (HPV) is a DNA virus from the Papillomaviridae family. Certain HPV types are associated with genital infections and are among the most common sexually transmitted pathogens globally. Some types are linked to the development of genital warts and cancers.

Characteristics

  • Non-enveloped, double-stranded DNA virus
  • Over 200 known types; classified as low-risk or high-risk based on oncogenic potential
  • Targets squamous epithelial cells of the genital, oral, and respiratory tracts
  • High-risk types (e.g., HPV-16, HPV-18) are associated with malignancies

Pathogenicity

HPV infects basal epithelial cells and promotes cell proliferation. Persistent infection with high-risk HPV types can result in dysplasia and malignant transformation, particularly in the cervix, penis, anus, and oropharynx. Low-risk types cause benign lesions such as genital warts.

Clinical Significance

HPV is a leading cause of cervical cancer and other anogenital malignancies. It also causes genital warts, which can affect the urethra and urinary tract. Screening (e.g., Pap smear) and vaccination (e.g., Gardasil, Cervarix) are important preventive measures.

Treatment

There is no antiviral treatment for HPV itself. Management includes removal of warts (cryotherapy, excision, topical agents) and monitoring for neoplastic changes. Vaccination is highly effective in preventing infection by common oncogenic and wart-causing HPV types.
